The Business Problem

In a clinical laboratory setting, Turnaround Time (TAT) — the time from sample collection to result reporting — is a critical quality indicator. Prolonged TAT delays diagnosis, disrupts clinical workflows, and negatively affects patient satisfaction. This project was built to answer: Where in the lab process are delays occurring, and what can be done to fix them? The goal was to provide actionable, data-backed recommendations to the laboratory management team.

Dataset

Laboratory transaction data including sample collection timestamps, processing stage timestamps, result release times, test type categories, and shift/operator records. Data was processed and cleaned using Python (pandas), with statistical analysis performed to detect outliers and compare TAT across test types, shifts, and time periods.

Analysis Process

1

Data Collection & Cleaning — Imported raw timestamp data from laboratory information system (LIS) exports. Removed duplicates, handled missing timestamps, and converted date fields for accurate TAT calculation.

2

TAT Calculation — Calculated TAT in minutes for each sample across multiple stages: pre-analytical (collection → receipt), analytical (receipt → result), and post-analytical (result → release). This breakdown enabled stage-specific bottleneck identification.

3

Statistical Analysis — Applied descriptive statistics, percentile analysis (P75, P90, P95), and outlier detection to understand TAT distribution. Used SPSS for normality testing and comparative analysis across test categories.

4

Bottleneck Identification — Segmented TAT data by test type, shift (morning/afternoon/night), day of week, and operator to identify which variables most strongly correlated with delayed results.

5

Visualization & Reporting — Created box plots, trend charts, and heatmaps using matplotlib and seaborn to communicate findings clearly to both technical and non-technical stakeholders.

Key Insights

Pre-analytical Stage is the Primary Bottleneck

Analysis revealed that the pre-analytical stage (sample collection to laboratory receipt) accounted for the largest share of total TAT — indicating that transportation and sample prioritization workflows are more impactful than the testing process itself.

Night Shift TAT is Significantly Higher

Statistical comparison across shifts showed that TAT on night shifts was measurably longer, likely due to reduced staffing and lower sample volume but slower batch processing patterns.

High-Volume Test Types Show Consistent Delays

Certain high-demand test categories (e.g., hematology and chemistry panels) consistently exceeded target TAT thresholds, suggesting a capacity or prioritization issue during peak hours.

Business Impact

The findings provided laboratory management with a clear prioritization framework: focus on pre-analytical transport efficiency first, review night-shift batching protocols second, and consider additional capacity for peak-volume test categories. If implemented, these recommendations could reduce average TAT by an estimated 15–25%, directly improving clinical decision speed and patient flow.
