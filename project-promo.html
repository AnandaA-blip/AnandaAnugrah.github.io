Retail Promo Markdown Effectiveness Analysis

Discounts cost money. Do they actually drive enough volume to justify the margin sacrifice? This project analyzes the true revenue lift from promotional markdown events, segments results by product category, and tells the business which promotions are working and which are not.

The Business Problem

A retail business regularly runs promotional markdown campaigns — but without clear measurement, there is no way to know if a discount event truly drives incremental revenue or simply moves purchases that would have happened anyway (demand cannibalization). The business question: Are our markdown promotions generating a positive revenue lift? Which product categories respond most strongly to price reductions? And are there periods or campaign types that consistently underperform? Answering these questions turns promotional spend from a cost center into a measurable investment.

Dataset

Retail sales transaction dataset containing weekly store-level sales data, promotional event flags, markdown discount levels by category, and baseline (non-promotion) sales periods. SQL was used for data extraction and aggregation, Python for analysis and visualization, and Tableau for the final reporting dashboard. The dataset structure is based on publicly available retail sales data modeled to reflect real-world promotional measurement scenarios.

Analysis Process

1

Baseline Establishment — Used SQL to extract sales data for matched control periods (non-promotional weeks) for each product category and store. Calculated average weekly revenue as a pre-promotion baseline.

2

Promotional Period Extraction — Identified promotional weeks by joining transaction data with markdown event flags. Calculated total revenue and units sold during each promotional window per category.

3

Lift Calculation — Computed incremental revenue lift as: (Promotional Period Revenue − Baseline Revenue) / Baseline Revenue × 100. Categorized results as positive lift, neutral, or negative lift (demand pull-forward or cannibalization).

4

Category Segmentation — Grouped lift analysis by product category and markdown depth (shallow: 10–20%, medium: 20–35%, deep: 35%+). Identified which categories showed the strongest demand elasticity.

5

Dashboard & Reporting — Built a Tableau dashboard showing promotion performance by category, markdown depth vs. lift scatter analysis, and a ranked promotion effectiveness table for management review.

Key Insights

Deep Discounts Do Not Always Drive the Highest Lift

Analysis showed that medium-depth markdowns (20–35%) produced the most consistent positive revenue lift. Deep discounts (35%+) in some categories generated volume but resulted in net margin erosion, suggesting diminishing returns at high discount levels.

Category Response to Promotion Varies Significantly

Some product categories are highly elastic — small price reductions drive large volume increases. Others show minimal demand response to discounting, meaning markdown spend in those categories is largely wasted.

Seasonality Interacts with Promotion Effectiveness

Promotional lift was significantly higher during high-traffic seasonal periods. Running deep discounts during low-traffic periods produced lower ROI, suggesting promotions should be timed to coincide with natural traffic peaks.

Business Impact

The findings provide a clear framework for optimizing promotional budget allocation: concentrate markdown investment in high-elasticity categories, use medium-depth discounts as the default strategy, and time promotions to high-traffic seasonal windows. This analysis can directly inform the promotional planning calendar and reduce ineffective markdown spend.
